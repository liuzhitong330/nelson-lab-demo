Why the Motor Striatum Hyperactivates: dMSN Subpopulation Heterogeneity in Levodopa-Induced Dyskinesia

The Nelson lab studies how striatal circuits generate normal and abnormal movement, with a focus on Parkinson's disease, levodopa-induced dyskinesia (LID), and the cellular logic of action selection in the basal ganglia. Their approach — combining activity-dependent cell labeling (FosTRAP), chemogenetics, optogenetics, and ex vivo electrophysiology — allows them to ask questions about specific, functionally defined cell populations rather than brain regions as a whole.

I was struck by the 2024 Cell Reports paper (Ryan et al.) showing that LID is driven not by all direct pathway MSNs (dMSNs), but by a specific subpopulation — TRAPed dMSNs — that have higher D1 receptor expression, greater dopamine-dependent excitability, and more excitatory input from motor cortex and thalamus. That raises a question the Allen Brain Atlas can partially address: what does the receptor expression landscape look like across the full motor circuit? Where does the combination of D1 sensitivity + glutamatergic drive actually peak?

Dorsal striatum: peak GluN2B expression, substantial D1/D2 co-presence

CP (dorsal striatum) has the highest Grin2b expression (0.144) of any region examined — including motor cortex (0.139) and nucleus accumbens (0.114). GluN2B-containing NMDA receptors mediate the glutamatergic drive from motor cortex and thalamus onto striatal MSNs. In the context of the Ryan 2024 paper, this matters because TRAPed dMSNs specifically receive more excitatory synaptic input from MOp and thalamus. The high Grin2b density in CP means those synapses are operating on a background of strong NMDA-receptor expression — the substrate for the dopamine-glutamate interaction that tips TRAPed dMSNs into LID-associated hyperactivation.
ACB has 7.6× more Drd1 than CP (0.105 vs. 0.014), but LID is a dorsal-striatum-specific phenomenon. The higher absolute receptor density in nucleus accumbens reflects its role in reward and motivation circuits, where phasic dopamine signals are more prominent. By contrast, dorsal striatum (CP) operates in a regime where D1 and D2 receptor populations are both present and balanced (D1/D2 ratio ≈ 0.57 in CP vs. 1.10 in ACB), so the dopamine-induced shift in direct vs. indirect pathway balance is largest and most sensitive to perturbation by levodopa. The Ryan et al. finding — that a subset of CP dMSNs with slightly higher Drd1 drives all of LID — makes sense in this context: in a region where D1 expression is lower and more heterogeneous, small differences in receptor density produce large functional differences.

The TRAPed dMSN: how a subpopulation drives dyskinesia

The key question the TRAPed dMSN model opens: are these cells constitutively different, or are they shaped by prior dopamine depletion? If TRAPed dMSNs have pre-existing higher Drd1 expression (intrinsic heterogeneity), then LID risk might be predictable from baseline striatal composition before dopamine depletion. If instead the upregulation of Drd1 and excitatory synaptic strength is driven by chronic dopamine loss (the 6-OHDA state), then LID is a learned circuit state — and its prevention might be possible by intervening earlier in the disease. The Twedell et al. 2024 (bioRxiv) paper from the same lab, showing that lateral inhibition from D2-MSNs gates the threshold for involuntary movements, adds another layer: whether lateral inhibition itself changes in the dopamine-depleted and levodopa-treated striatum could determine why some patients develop LID and others don't.

Connection to the lab's work

  • TRAPed dMSN identity and plasticity: The expression data suggests CP has higher Grin2b than any other region — a substrate for Hebbian strengthening of cortical inputs onto TRAPed dMSNs. I'd be excited to contribute to experiments asking whether synaptic potentiation at MOp → TRAPed dMSN synapses is necessary for LID expression, or whether blocking GluN2B-containing NMDARs specifically in TRAPed cells alters dyskinesia.
  • Lateral inhibition and action selection: The Twedell 2024 work on striatal D2-MSN lateral inhibition raises the question of whether lateral inhibition from D2-MSNs specifically suppresses TRAPed dMSN activity, and whether this connection is weakened by chronic dopamine loss. The combination of the FosTRAP approach with circuit-specific chemogenetics seems well suited to test this.
  • On the experimental side, I have hands-on experience with mammalian cell culture, Western blot, ELISA, flow cytometry, and fluorescence microscopy. I'm also comfortable with computational analysis of expression and circuit data and would be glad to contribute on both sides.
